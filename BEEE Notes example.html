Synchronous Speed & Slip — 1-mark numericals🔴 Repeated 2× PYQs
CGC 2025 · Sec A (a)⭐ REPEATED
A 3-phase induction motor has 30 poles and is connected to a 50 Hz AC supply. Find the synchronous speed of the Rotating Magnetic Field (RMF) in the motor.
✅ Exam Answer

Using the formula Ns = 120f / P, we get Ns = 120 × 50 / 30 = 200 rpm. Therefore the synchronous speed of the RMF is 200 rpm.

CEC Nov 2024 · Sec A (a)⭐ REPEATED
The synchronous speed of an induction motor is 1500 r.p.m and the rotor speed is 1440 r.p.m. Interpret the slip.
✅ Exam Answer

Using the formula s = (Ns − Nr) / Ns × 100, we get s = (1500 − 1440) / 1500 × 100 = 4%. Therefore the slip of the induction motor is 4%.

MCB, ELCB & MCCB — 1-mark questions🔴 Repeated 3× PYQs
CGC 2025 · Sec A (b)⭐ REPEATED
List the primary functions of a Miniature Circuit Breaker (MCB).
✅ Exam Answer

An MCB serves four main functions. First, it provides overload protection by tripping automatically when excess current flows continuously. Second, it provides short circuit protection by tripping instantly when a sudden large fault current occurs. Third, it works as a manual switch that can be turned ON or OFF. Fourth, it is reusable — after a trip, it can simply be reset by hand, unlike a fuse which must be replaced.

CEC Apr 2025 · Sec A (a)⭐ REPEATED
Illustrate the usage of ELCB.
✅ Exam Answer

An ELCB (Earth Leakage Circuit Breaker) is used to protect humans from electric shock. It detects any leakage of current to earth, even as small as 30 mA, and trips the circuit within 30 milliseconds — which is fast enough to prevent a fatal shock. It is installed in homes, bathrooms, kitchens, swimming pool areas, and any other location where the risk of electric shock is high.

CEC Apr 2025 · Sec A (b)
Which breaker can handle higher current capacities — MCB or MCCB?
✅ Exam Answer

MCCB (Moulded Case Circuit Breaker) handles much higher current than MCB. An MCB is rated up to 100A and is used in homes and commercial buildings. An MCCB is rated up to 1600A and is used in industrial plants and main distribution boards. Additionally, MCCB has adjustable trip settings while MCB does not.

CGC 2025 · Sec A (a)

✅ Exam Answer

An SFU (Switch Fuse Unit) is a combination of a manual isolating switch and a fuse in one unit. LVDT & Transducer — 1-mark questions🔴 Repeated 3× PYQs
CEC Apr 2025 · Sec A (c)⭐ REPEATED
Interpret the characteristic curve of an LVDT.
✅ Exam Answer

The LVDT characteristic curve is a straight line passing through the origin. The X-axis shows core displacement and the Y-axis shows output voltage. When the core is at the centre (null position), the output voltage is zero. When the core moves to the right, the output voltage becomes positive and increases proportionally with distance. When the core moves to the left, the output voltage becomes negative. The straight portion in the middle is called the linear range, which is the useful operating region of the LVDT. Beyond this region, the curve bends and enters saturation.

CGC 2025 · Sec A (c–d) / CEC Nov 2024 · Sec A (e)⭐ REPEATED
List some applications of the Linear Variable Differential Transformer (LVDT).
✅ Exam Answer

LVDT is used in many practical applications. It is used for displacement measurement in CNC machines to track the position of a cutting tool. It measures pressure by detecting the deflection of a diaphragm caused by pressure. It measures force or load by detecting how much a spring compresses. It is also used for liquid level measurement by tracking a float that rises with the liquid. In aircraft, it measures the position of flaps and control surfaces.

CEC Nov 2024 · Sec A (d)⭐ REPEATED
Define transducer and give an example.
✅ Exam Answer

A transducer is a device that converts one form of energy into another. In instrumentation, it converts a physical quantity such as displacement, pressure, or temperature into a proportional electrical signal like voltage or current, so that it can be measured or used for control. For example, an LVDT converts linear displacement into AC voltage, and a thermocouple converts temperature difference into voltage.

Multimeter & Power Flow — 1-mark questions🔵 Repeated 2–3× PYQs
CGC 2025 · Sec A (e)⭐ REPEATED
Infer the electrical parameters measured using a digital multimeter.
✅ Exam Answer

A digital multimeter measures DC voltage, AC voltage, DC current, and resistance. It also performs continuity testing, where it beeps if a wire or connection is unbroken. Advanced models can also measure capacitance, frequency, and test diodes.

CEC Apr 2025 · Sec A (e)⭐ REPEATED
Demonstrate the power flow in an induction motor.
✅ Exam Answer

Power flows through an induction motor in stages. The input electrical power enters the stator, where stator copper loss (I²R) and stator iron loss (core loss) are subtracted. The remaining power crosses the air gap magnetically and reaches the rotor — this is called the air gap power (Pg). From this, rotor copper loss (equal to slip × Pg) is subtracted. What remains is the mechanical power developed, which equals (1 − slip) × Pg. After subtracting friction and windage losses, the final shaft output power is obtained, which does useful work.

MCB — Miniature Circuit Breaker🔴 4/4 PYQs · GUARANTEED
CEC Apr 2025 · Sec B Q2⭐ 5 MARKS
Illustrate the construction and working of a MCB with a labeled diagram.
✅ Exam Answer — Write This (5 Marks)

An MCB (Miniature Circuit Breaker) is an automatic switching device that protects electrical circuits from damage caused by overload and short circuit currents. Unlike a fuse, which melts and needs replacement, an MCB trips automatically and can be reset manually, making it reusable.

Construction: [Draw labeled diagram here — see diagram box below.] The main parts are a bimetallic strip, a solenoid or electromagnet coil, a trip latch, fixed and moving contacts, an arc-extinguishing chamber, and a toggle switch on the outside.

Thermal Protection (for overload — slow action): Thermal protection is used when the current is moderately higher than normal for a longer time. It uses a bimetallic strip made of two metals such as brass and steel that are bonded together. When excess current flows, the strip heats up and bends because the two metals expand at different rates. This bending pushes the trip latch, which opens the moving contact away from the fixed contact, breaking the circuit. This action takes a few seconds to a few minutes and is designed for gradual overloads like a motor running under too much load.

Magnetic Protection (for short circuit — instant action): Magnetic protection is used when a sudden, very large fault current flows due to a short circuit. It uses a solenoid or electromagnet coil that is connected in series with the circuit. During a short circuit, the heavy fault current makes the electromagnet very strong, which instantly pulls the plunger and trips the contacts in less than 10 milliseconds. This fast action protects equipment from severe damage before any heat can build up.

Types: Type B trips at 3–5 times rated current and is used in homes for lighting. Type C trips at 5–10 times and is used in commercial buildings and small motors. Type D trips at 10–20 times and is used in industrial settings with large motors.

Write the full 5-mark answer above, then add this additional content for 10 marks:

Why MCB is better than a fuse: A fuse works on the same principle — it melts when excess current flows — but once it melts, it must be physically replaced. An MCB, on the other hand, simply trips and can be switched back on by hand. This makes MCB faster, more reliable, and more economical in the long run. It also has a clearly visible ON/OFF state, which is helpful during fault diagnosis.

Arc Extinguishing: When the contacts open, a small arc forms between them due to the high voltage. The arc-extinguishing chamber inside the MCB uses metal arc splitter plates to divide and cool this arc, extinguishing it quickly and preventing damage to the contacts.

ELCB — Earth Leakage Circuit Breaker🔴 4/4 PYQs · GUARANTEED
CGC 2025 · Sec B Q2⭐ 5 MARKS
Illustrate the construction and working of an ELCB with a labeled diagram. Describe its function in electrical safety.
✅ Exam Answer — Write This (5 Marks)

An ELCB (Earth Leakage Circuit Breaker), also called an RCD (Residual Current Device), is a safety device that protects humans from fatal electric shocks. A current of just 30 mA passing through the human body can cause cardiac arrest, which takes less than 30 milliseconds to become fatal. An MCB cannot detect such small currents, so ELCB is specifically designed for human protection.

Construction: [Draw labeled diagram — see diagram box below.] The key part is a toroidal (donut-shaped) iron core through which both the Live wire and the Neutral wire pass. A sensing coil is wound around this toroid. The output of the sensing coil is connected to a relay that controls the trip contacts.

Working — Normal Condition: In a healthy circuit, the current going out through the Live wire is exactly equal to the current returning through the Neutral wire. Since both wires pass through the toroid in opposite directions, their magnetic fluxes cancel each other completely. The sensing coil gets zero output, so the relay stays off and the circuit remains connected.

Working — Fault Condition: If a person accidentally touches a live part, some current flows through their body to earth instead of returning through the Neutral. This means the Live current becomes greater than the Neutral current, so the fluxes no longer cancel. The net flux in the toroid induces an EMF in the sensing coil, which energises the relay and opens the trip contacts — all within less than 30 milliseconds, which is fast enough to save the person's life.

Trip settings: In residential installations, ELCB trips at 30 mA leakage to protect against electric shock. In industrial settings, it trips at 300 mA to protect against fire caused by leakage currents.

Earthing — Objectives, Types & Diagrams🔴 4/4 PYQs · GUARANTEED
CEC Apr 2025 · Sec B Q2 (OR)⭐ 5 MARKS
Outline the necessity of Earthing in electrical installation. Also state different types of earthing.
✅ Exam Answer — Write This (5 Marks)

Earthing means connecting the metallic parts of electrical equipment — such as a motor body, switchgear casing, or transformer tank — to the earth through a low-resistance path. The earth is always at zero potential, so it acts as a safe discharge point for any unwanted fault current.

Necessity of Earthing: The most important reason for earthing is human safety. If a live wire accidentally touches the body of a machine, and a person also touches that machine, a dangerous current will flow through the person. With proper earthing, the fault current flows to earth through the earthing conductor instead of through the person. Earthing also helps clear faults quickly, because a large earth fault current trips the MCB or fuse. It stabilises voltages by keeping equipment bodies at zero potential, provides a safe path for lightning to discharge, and is legally mandatory under IS 3043 and the Indian Electricity Rules.

Types of Earthing: The two main methods are Plate Earthing and Pipe Earthing. Strip or Wire Earthing is also used in some cases.

Plate Earthing: A copper or galvanised iron plate is buried vertically in the earth at a minimum depth of 3 metres. The copper plate is 600 mm × 600 mm × 3.18 mm and the GI plate is 600 mm × 600 mm × 6.35 mm. The plate is surrounded by alternating layers of charcoal and salt, which reduce soil resistivity and keep resistance low. An earth wire connects the plate to the equipment body. A watering pipe and inspection chamber are built at the surface so the earth electrode can be checked and kept moist.

Pipe Earthing: A GI pipe of 38 mm diameter and 2.5 metres length is buried vertically in the earth. Small holes are drilled along the pipe's length, which allow moisture from the surrounding soil to enter the pipe and maintain a low resistance even during dry seasons. Charcoal and salt are packed around the pipe. An earth wire is connected at the top of the pipe, and an inspection chamber is provided at ground level.

Earth resistance: The ideal resistance is less than 1 Ω. The maximum allowed value as per IS 3043 is 5 Ω.

LVDT — Linear Variable Differential Transformer🔴 4/4 PYQs · GUARANTEED
CEC Apr 2025 · Sec B Q3 (OR)⭐ 5 MARKS
Demonstrate the construction and working of LVDT with the help of a suitable neat sketch.
✅ Exam Answer — Write This (5 Marks)

LVDT stands for Linear Variable Differential Transformer. It is an electrical device used to measure linear displacement, which means straight line movement.

Construction: [Draw labeled diagram — see diagram box below.] It has one primary coil P placed in the center, with two secondary coils S1 and S2 on either side. The primary coil is connected to AC supply. S1 and S2 are connected in series opposition, so Vo = VS1 − VS2. A soft iron movable core is placed inside the coils. This core is attached to the object whose movement is to be measured.

Working — Core at Center Position: When the core is exactly at the center, equal voltage is induced in S1 and S2. Since both coils are in series opposition, Vo = VS1 − VS2 = 0 volts. This is called the null position.

Working — Core Moves Toward S1: When the core moves toward S1, more voltage is induced in S1 and less in S2. So VS1 > VS2, and the output voltage Vo becomes positive. More the core moves, higher the output.

Working — Core Moves Toward S2: When the core moves toward S2, more voltage is induced in S2 and less in S1. So VS2 > VS1, and Vo becomes negative. The output increases in the opposite direction.

Since the output voltage depends on the position of the core, the amount of displacement can be measured accurately.

Output characteristic: [Draw the graph — see box below.] The graph is a straight line passing through the origin. The middle straight portion is the useful range. At both ends it curves into saturation.

CEC (partial) · Sec C Q8⭐ 10 MARKS
Explain the concept of LVDT and its working principle with the help of a clear and appropriate sketch.
✅ Extra Content for 10 Marks

Write the full 5-mark answer above, then add the following:

Advantages of LVDT: Resolution is infinite — even the smallest movement produces an output. There is no friction because the core never touches the coil, so the device lasts very long. The output is directly proportional to displacement, so calibration is simple. There is no hysteresis — it gives the same reading for the same position from any direction. It can work in rough conditions like vibration, shock, and high temperatures.

Applications: It is used in CNC machines to track the position of the cutting tool. It measures pressure by detecting how much a diaphragm bends under pressure. It measures force by tracking how much a spring compresses. It is used in liquid level gauges where a float rises with the liquid. In aircraft, it measures the position of control surfaces like flaps.

Disadvantage: LVDT can only measure straight line (linear) displacement. For rotary displacement, RVDT is used instead. Also, the output is AC voltage, so a demodulator circuit is needed if DC output is required.

Transducers — Definition, Types, Classification🔵 3/4 PYQs
CEC Apr 2025 · Sec B Q3⭐ 5 MARKS
Explain the working principle of primary and secondary transducer and provide one relevant example for each.
✅ Exam Answer — Write This (5 Marks)

A transducer is a device that converts one form of energy into another. In electrical measurements, it converts a physical quantity like displacement, pressure, temperature, or force into an electrical signal like voltage or current.

Primary Transducer: A primary transducer is the first stage of sensing. It directly senses the physical quantity being measured and converts it into a mechanical output such as displacement or force. It does not produce an electrical output on its own. A Bourdon tube is a good example — when pressure is applied, the curved tube straightens slightly, and its tip moves by a small amount. This mechanical displacement of the tip is the primary transducer's output.

Secondary Transducer: A secondary transducer takes the mechanical output of the primary transducer and converts it into an electrical signal. An LVDT is a good example — it takes the mechanical displacement from the tip of the Bourdon tube and converts it into a proportional AC voltage that can be measured on a meter or recorder.

Together, the Bourdon tube (primary) and LVDT (secondary) form a complete pressure measurement system. The primary transducer senses the quantity, and the secondary transducer makes it measurable electrically.

CEC (partial) · Sec C Q8 (OR)⭐ 10 MARKS
Provide a detailed explanation of the term transducer, supported by a block diagram. Classify transducers into five distinct categories and provide a relevant example for each.
✅ Exam Answer — Write This (10 Marks)

Write the 5-mark answer above first. Then add the block diagram and 5-type classification below.

Block Diagram: [Draw: Physical Input → Sensing Element → Transduction Element → Electrical Output → Signal Conditioning → Display or Control System. Add an example below: Pressure → Bourdon tube → LVDT → Voltage → Amplifier → Digital display.]

Type 1 — Resistive Transducers: These change their electrical resistance when a physical quantity is applied. A strain gauge is a common example. When force is applied, the wire inside stretches and its resistance increases. This resistance change is measured and converted into a force or stress value. A thermistor is another example where resistance changes with temperature. These are passive transducers and need an external power supply.

Type 2 — Inductive Transducers: These change their inductance when the physical input changes. An LVDT is the best example. As the iron core moves, the mutual inductance between the primary and secondary coils changes, giving a proportional output voltage. These also need external excitation.

Type 3 — Capacitive Transducers: These change their capacitance. When two conducting plates move closer or farther apart, the capacitance between them changes. This principle is used to measure displacement, liquid level, and humidity. These are also passive transducers.

Type 4 — Piezoelectric Transducers: Certain crystals like quartz produce a voltage when mechanical stress is applied to them. This is called the piezoelectric effect. An accelerometer uses this to measure vibration and acceleration. Microphones and ultrasonic sensors also work on this principle. These are active transducers and generate their own output without external supply.

Type 5 — Thermoelectric Transducers: When two different metals are joined at a point and that junction is heated, a voltage is produced. This is called the Seebeck effect. A thermocouple works on this principle and is used to measure temperature in furnaces, engines, and medical equipment. These are also active transducers.

Digital Multimeter + Analog vs Digital Comparison🔵 3/4 PYQs
CGC 2025 · Sec B Q3⭐ 5 MARKS
Interpret the working principle of a digital multimeter with the help of a neat block diagram.
✅ Exam Answer — Write This (5 Marks)

A Digital Multimeter (DMM) is an electronic measuring instrument that can measure DC voltage, AC voltage, DC current, resistance, and continuity, displaying the result as a numeric value on an LCD screen.

Block Diagram: [Draw: Input terminals → Range Selector / Input Conditioning → A/D Converter → Digital Processor / Microcontroller → LCD Display. Also draw a simple front panel below showing the LCD at the top, a large rotary selector dial in the middle with positions for DCV, ACV, DCA, Ω, and Continuity, and four input sockets at the bottom: COM, VΩ, mA, and 10A.]

Working: When the test probes are connected to the circuit, the unknown quantity enters through the input terminals. The range selector adjusts the signal to a level that the internal electronics can handle safely. The Analogue-to-Digital (A/D) Converter is the heart of the DMM — it samples the analogue input signal many times per second and converts each sample into a binary number. The microprocessor then processes this binary data and sends it to the LCD display, which shows the final reading with the correct units, such as "12.5 V DC" or "470 Ω".

Measurements possible: DC Voltage, AC Voltage, DC Current, Resistance, Continuity (beeps if circuit is complete), Diode testing, and in advanced models, Capacitance and Frequency.

CEC Nov 2024 · Sec B Q4⭐ 5 MARKS
Differentiate between the Analog and Digital multimeters.
✅ Exam Answer — Write This (5 Marks)

An analog multimeter uses a moving pointer on a printed scale to show measurements, while a digital multimeter shows the result as a number on an LCD or seven-segment display. Both devices measure voltage, current, and resistance, but they work very differently and are suited to different situations.

3-Phase Induction Motor — Working, Construction, Torque-Slip🔵 3/4 PYQs
CEC Apr 2025 · Sec B Q4⭐ 5 MARKS
Illustrate three phase induction motor with the help of its working principle and construction.
✅ Exam Answer — Write This (5 Marks)

A three-phase induction motor is the most widely used electric motor in industry. It runs on three-phase AC supply and does not need any brushes or slip rings in its basic form, making it simple, rugged, and low-maintenance.

Working Principle: When three-phase AC supply is given to the stator winding, it creates a Rotating Magnetic Field (RMF) that rotates at synchronous speed Ns = 120f/P rpm. As this rotating field passes over the stationary rotor conductors, it cuts through them and induces an EMF in the rotor by Faraday's law. Since the rotor conductors are short-circuited, this EMF drives a current through them. These current-carrying rotor conductors are now inside a magnetic field, so by the BIL law, a force acts on them, causing the rotor to rotate. The rotor always rotates slightly slower than the RMF because if both were at the same speed, there would be no relative motion, no induced EMF, no current, and no force. So a small speed difference, called slip, must always exist.

Slip: Slip is defined as s = (Ns − Nr) / Ns × 100%. At full load, normal slip is between 2% and 8%.

Construction — Stator: The stator is the outer stationary part. It consists of a laminated silicon steel core with slots cut into it. Three-phase windings are placed in these slots, physically separated by 120° in space. When three-phase supply is applied, these windings create the Rotating Magnetic Field.

Construction — Rotor: The most common rotor type is the squirrel cage rotor. It has aluminium or copper bars placed in slots on the rotor core, with both ends of the bars connected by end rings, forming a cage-like structure. There are no external connections. This makes it very simple, robust, and cheap. A wound rotor type also exists, which uses external resistors connected through slip rings for speed control and high starting torque applications.

CGC 2025 · Sec B Q4⭐ 5 MARKS
Contrast the torque slip characteristics of a three-phase Induction motor.
✅ Exam Answer — Torque-Slip Curve

[Draw the torque-slip curve — see diagram box below.]

The torque-slip characteristic shows how the torque produced by a three-phase induction motor changes as the slip changes from 0 to 1.

At s = 0 (synchronous speed, no load): There is no relative motion between the rotor and the rotating field, so no EMF is induced, no current flows, and no torque is produced. The torque is zero.

Stable region (s = 0 to sm): As the load on the motor increases, the motor slows down slightly, increasing the slip. This increases the induced EMF and rotor current, which increases torque. In this region, torque increases nearly linearly with slip. This is where the motor normally operates — at small values of slip near zero. When the load increases, the motor automatically produces more torque to compensate.

Maximum torque (pull-out torque) at s = sm: As slip increases further, the rotor reactance becomes significant and limits the current. The torque reaches a maximum value called the pull-out torque or breakdown torque at a slip value of sm (typically 0.1 to 0.3). This is the highest torque the motor can produce. If the load exceeds this value, the motor will stall.

Unstable region (s = sm to 1): Beyond the maximum torque point, as slip increases further, the torque actually decreases. This region is unstable — if the motor enters it, it cannot recover and will come to a stop. The motor must not be operated here.

At s = 1 (standstill, starting): At startup, the rotor is stationary. The torque produced at s = 1 is called the starting torque. For the motor to start successfully, this starting torque must be greater than the load torque.

A 220/400 V, 10 kVA, 50 Hz, single-phase transformer has a copper loss of 120 W at full load. If it has an efficiency of 98% at full load and unity power factor, find the iron losses. Also find the efficiency at half full load at 0.8 p.f. lagging.
✅ Step-by-Step Solution

Given: kVA = 10, PCu_FL = 120 W, η_FL = 98% = 0.98, pf_FL = 1 (unity), pf_half = 0.8

Part (a) — Find Iron Loss (Pi): Full load output power = kVA × pf = 10,000 × 1 = 10,000 W. Using the efficiency formula: 0.98 = 10,000 / (10,000 + 120 + Pi). Rearranging: 10,000 + 120 + Pi = 10,000 / 0.98 = 10,204 W. Therefore Pi = 10,204 − 10,000 − 120 = 84 W. Iron loss = 84 W.

Part (b) — Efficiency at half load, 0.8 pf: Copper loss at half load = (0.5)² × 120 = 0.25 × 120 = 30 W. Output power at half load = 0.5 × 10,000 × 0.8 = 4,000 W. Total losses = Pi + PCu_half = 84 + 30 = 114 W. η = 4,000 / (4,000 + 114) × 100 = 4,000 / 4,114 × 100 = 97.23%

Auto-Transformer — Construction, Working, Copper Saving🔵 2/4 PYQs
CEC (partial) · Sec B Q6⭐ 5 MARKS
Discuss the construction of an auto-transformer and infer the expression for the copper savings in it.
✅ Exam Answer — Write This (5 Marks)

In a normal two-winding transformer, the primary and secondary are two completely separate and electrically isolated windings. All power is transferred magnetically across the core. An auto-transformer, by contrast, uses only a single winding with a tapping point somewhere along it. The part of the winding above the tap serves as the primary, and the part from the tap to one end serves as the secondary. The primary and secondary are therefore electrically connected and share the same winding.

Construction: [Draw diagram — see box below.] The single winding is wound on a laminated iron core. The full winding of N1 turns is connected to the input supply V1. A tap is taken at N2 turns from one end, and the output V2 is taken between this tap and the same end. The ratio V1/V2 = N1/N2 = 1/k, where k = V2/V1 is called the turns ratio.

Copper Saving: In a two-winding transformer, both the primary and secondary windings carry their full respective currents through their full length, requiring a certain amount of copper. In an auto-transformer, the section of winding between the tap and the end is shared by both primary and secondary circuits. This shared section only carries the difference current (I2 − I1) rather than the full current, so it needs much less copper. The copper saved, expressed as a fraction of the copper in a two-winding transformer, is equal to k = V2/V1. The closer V2 is to V1 (i.e., k close to 1), the more copper is saved. This is why auto-transformers are most useful for small voltage differences such as 400V to 440V.

Key Formulas
Turns ratio: k = V2 / V1 (k < 1 for step-down)
Copper saving fraction = k
kVA of auto-TF = kVA of two-winding TF / (1 − k)

Advantages: Less copper, lower cost, lighter weight, higher efficiency, and better voltage regulation compared to a two-winding transformer. Disadvantage: There is no electrical isolation between input and output, which is a safety concern. It cannot be used where isolation is required.

CGC 2025 · Sec B Q4 (OR)⭐ 5 MARKS
Extract the method of connecting a 400/100 V, 5 kVA two-winding transformer as an autotransformer to obtain 400 V output from a 500 V supply. Determine the kVA output of the autotransformer.
✅ Exam Answer — Write This (5 Marks)

Connection method: The 400V primary winding and the 100V secondary winding are connected in series in an additive (boost) manner. The 400V winding receives 400V from part of the supply, and the 100V winding receives the remaining 100V, so together they handle 500V input. The 400V winding terminals are used as the output, giving 400V output.

Turns ratio of the auto-transformer: k = V2 / V1 = 400 / 500 = 0.8

kVA output: kVA_auto = kVA_two-winding / (1 − k) = 5 / (1 − 0.8) = 5 / 0.2 = 25 kVA

The same transformer that was rated 5 kVA as a two-winding transformer can now deliver 25 kVA when reconnected as an auto-transformer. This five-fold increase in capacity is the key advantage — because part of the power is transferred by direct electrical conduction through the shared winding rather than purely by magnetic induction.

Wires & Cables — Types, Properties, Applications🔴 3–4/4 PYQs
CGC 2025 · Sec B Q2 (OR)⭐ 5 MARKS
Classify the different types of electrical wires based on their properties and their suitability for various applications.
✅ Exam Answer — Write This (5 Marks)

A wire is a single conductor that may be bare or insulated. A cable is a group of two or more conductors bundled together with insulation. Cables are used for power distribution, while wires are used for internal connections within panels and boards.

PVC Insulated Cable: This is the most widely used cable in homes and commercial buildings. Its insulation is made of PVC (Polyvinyl Chloride), which is cheap, flexible, and easy to install. It can operate at temperatures up to 70°C and is available in 2-core (Live + Neutral), 3-core (Live + Neutral + Earth), and 4-core versions. It is used for general domestic wiring, switches, sockets, and lighting circuits.

XLPE Cable (Cross-Linked Polyethylene): This cable uses XLPE insulation, which can withstand higher temperatures up to 90°C. Because of the higher temperature rating, the same size of XLPE cable can carry more current than a PVC cable. It also has better chemical resistance and is more fire-resistant. It is used in industrial installations, high-voltage underground distribution, and places where higher reliability is needed.

SWA Cable (Steel Wire Armoured): This cable has a standard PVC or XLPE insulated core, surrounded by a layer of steel wire armour. The armour protects the cable from mechanical damage such as digging, crushing, or rodent attack. It is used for underground installations, outdoor wiring in industrial sites, and wherever cables are exposed to physical hazards.

Coaxial Cable: A coaxial cable has an inner conductor, surrounded by insulation, then an outer cylindrical conductor (shield), and finally an outer jacket. The outer shield blocks electromagnetic interference from entering the signal. It is used for TV signals, older broadband internet connections, and radio frequency (RF) communications.

ACSR Conductor (Aluminium Conductor Steel Reinforced): This is the overhead conductor used on high-voltage transmission towers. It has a central steel core for mechanical strength, with aluminium strands wound around it for electrical conductivity. Aluminium is lightweight and a good conductor, while steel provides the tensile strength needed to span long distances between towers.

CEC Apr 2025 · Sec C Q5(a)⭐ 10 MARKS
Explain wires and cables in detail.
✅ Extra Content for 10 Marks

Write the full 5-mark answer above, then add the following:

Wire types in detail: A solid wire has a single, thick conductor and is used in fixed installations like electrical boards and conduit wiring. A stranded wire is made of many thin wires twisted together, which makes it flexible and suitable for use in portable equipment, flexible cords, and motors. An earth wire is typically green-yellow in colour and connects the metallic body of equipment to the earth electrode.

How to select a cable: The selection of a cable depends on several factors. The voltage rating of the cable must be higher than the supply voltage (for example, 1.1 kV rated cable for 230V or 415V domestic and industrial supply). The current rating must be higher than the expected load current so the cable does not overheat. The temperature of the environment determines whether PVC or XLPE insulation is needed. If the cable will be exposed to mechanical damage or buried underground, armoured cable (SWA) must be used. The number of cores is chosen based on the system: 2-core for single-phase, 3-core or 4-core for three-phase systems.

SFU + MCCB — 1-mark only⚪ 1–2/4 PYQs · Sec A only

An SFU (Switch Fuse Unit) is a combination of a manual isolating switch and a fuse in a single unit. Its main job is to provide visible isolation — when the switch is opened, it is clear to everyone that the equipment is safely disconnected from the supply, making maintenance safe. The built-in fuse provides overcurrent protection. However, unlike an MCB, it cannot reset itself — the fuse must be replaced after it blows. It is used as the incoming device before a distribution board and is rated up to 800A.

An MCCB (Moulded Case Circuit Breaker) is the industrial version of an MCB. While MCB is rated up to 100A for homes and offices, MCCB handles currents from 100A up to 1600A for industrial plants and main distribution boards. Its trip settings — both thermal (overload) and magnetic (short circuit) — are adjustable, which allows it to be tuned for different load types. Like an MCB, it is reusable and trips automatically.

Batteries — 1-mark only⚪ 1/4 PYQs

Batteries are classified as primary (non-rechargeable) or secondary (rechargeable). Primary batteries include dry cells (1.5V, used in remotes), alkaline batteries (1.5V, longer life), and lithium primary cells (3V, used in watches). Secondary batteries include lead-acid (12V, used in cars and UPS systems), Ni-Cd (1.2V per cell, used in power tools but suffers from memory effect), and Li-ion (3.6V per cell, used in phones and laptops with the best energy density). The capacity of a battery is measured in Ampere-hours (Ah). A 100 Ah battery can supply 100A for 1 hour, or 10A for 10 hours.
